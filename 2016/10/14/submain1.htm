Lawrenceville Student Unironically Thinks People Could Care About Literature

By Katherine Xiong '19

October 14th, 2016

LAWRENCEVILLE - A Lawrenceville student has recently been revealed to believe that literature is not only important in modern life, but that people could derive more than passing interest from the literary world.

“I just think that it's worthwhile,” said the anonymous student, aged 15. “It's just so full of new perspectives on the world, and I think as Lawrentians we are collectively capable of reading at least 1.5 real books a year instead of Sparknoting everything.

“Besides it's fun to stay caught up on all the newest literary gossip.”

Analysts from the Bunn Library have debunked her claims, calling them “completely unfounded”. They cited the number of books checked out from the library last year: zero. Meanwhile, library computer search histories show that searches for “sparknotes” and “how to get away with not reading books for class” have skyrocketed, and library proctors have reportedly had to guide students to the exits because they were unable to read the words on the library map.

In addition, results from a recent school-wide poll show an overall decrease in awareness of literary news - from 0.05% of the student population to 0.1%. In an open-response section to this survey, students were asked about recent literary scandals, including the reveal of pseudonymous author Elena Ferrante’s real name or the awarding of the Nobel Prize in literature to songwriter and groove master Bob Dylan. Responses ranged from “isn't that one of the ladies who works at Irwin?” to “who is Bob Dylan; is he going to be on our history final?” One especially outspoken student, fed up with the amount of literary articles being posted to Facebook, responded, “who the hell cares?”

Despite these disappointing responses, this student remains positive about the state of the literary future. “I’m sure most people will learn to love literature,” she said, adjusting her hipster glasses and readying a Snapstory of her kale-acai berry smoothie. “I’ve always been good at reading the trends. Last year, it was quinoa. This year, it’ll be reading. Sooner or later everyone succumbs to the literati.”

Asked if she would bail on reading, as she did with quinoa once it was served in Irwin, when it inevitably “goes mainstream”, the student declined to comment and left hurriedly left Starbucks.
